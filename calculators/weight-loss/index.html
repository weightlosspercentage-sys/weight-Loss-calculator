Getting your personalized weight loss plan takes under 60 seconds:

  1. Enter your current weight — toggle between lbs and kg.

  3. Add your height and age — these determine your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR).

The Science Behind Your Results

This calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation — the most accurate formula for estimating resting energy expenditure, validated in peer-reviewed research and recommended by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics.

How Your Calorie Target Is Calculated

  1. BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate): How many calories your body burns at complete rest — just to keep your heart beating, lungs breathing, and organs functioning.
  2. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure): Your BMR multiplied by an activity factor — the actual calories you burn each day.
  3. Calorie Deficit: Eat less than your TDEE. A deficit of 500 cal/day ≈ 1 lb of fat lost per week. A 1,000 cal/day deficit ≈ 2 lbs/week.

The 3,500-Calorie Rule — And Its Limits

Cutting 3,500 calories ≈ losing 1 pound is a useful estimate, but the NIDDK confirms real-world weight loss is not perfectly linear. As you lose weight, your TDEE decreases — which is why results slow down over time. This calculator accounts for that adaptation.

Safe Rate of Weight Loss

The CDC recommends losing 0.5 to 2 pounds (0.25–1 kg) per week. This range:

  • Preserves muscle mass (critical for long-term metabolism)
  • Is sustainable without extreme hunger
  • Reduces the risk of nutrient deficiencies
  • Produces results that are far more likely to last

What Is Weight Loss Percentage — And Why Does It Matter?

Raw pounds lost tells only part of the story. Weight loss percentage shows how much of your starting body weight you've lost — and it's the metric doctors and clinical researchers actually use.

The Formula

Weight Loss % = ((Starting Weight − Current Weight) ÷ Starting Weight) × 100

Clinical Benchmarks

  • 5% body weight loss — Measurable improvements in blood pressure, blood sugar, and cholesterol (CDC-recognized threshold)
  • 10% body weight loss — Significant reduction in risk for Type 2 diabetes, sleep apnea, and cardiovascular disease
  • 15–20% loss — Often required for meaningful joint pain relief and sleep apnea resolution

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate my weight loss percentage?

Subtract your current weight from your starting weight, divide by your starting weight, then multiply by 100. Example: starting at 200 lbs, now 185 lbs → ((200−185)÷200)×100 = 7.5%. Or use our step-by-step guide.

How many calories do I need to cut to lose 1 pound per week?

A deficit of approximately 500 calories per day (3,500 calories/week) produces roughly 1 lb of fat loss per week. Actual results vary based on your starting weight, metabolism, and muscle mass. Use the calculator above for your personalized number.

What is a healthy rate of weight loss?

The CDC recommends 0.5 to 2 pounds per week. Faster loss increases the risk of muscle loss, nutrient deficiencies, and weight regain.

How long does it take to lose 20 pounds?

At 1 lb/week: approximately 20 weeks (5 months). At 1.5 lbs/week: about 13–14 weeks. Enter your stats in the calculator above to get your exact personalized timeline.

Why did my weight loss stall?

As you lose weight, your TDEE decreases — your body burns fewer calories doing the same activities. This is normal. Reassess your calorie target every 4–6 weeks using this calculator with your updated current weight.

Is a 1,000 calorie deficit per day safe?

A 1,000 calorie daily deficit (≈2 lbs/week) is at the upper limit of what is clinically considered safe for most adults and should only be pursued under medical supervision for people with BMI over 30. For most people, 500–750 cal/day deficit is both effective and sustainable.

What is the difference between BMR and TDEE?

BMR is the calories your body burns at complete rest. TDEE is your BMR multiplied by your activity level — the actual calories you burn each day. Your weight loss target is always calculated from TDEE, not BMR.

Should I track calories or just eat healthy?

Both approaches work, but research shows that awareness of calorie intake significantly improves weight loss outcomes. You don't need to count every calorie forever, but understanding your approximate intake during the initial phase helps calibrate habits for long-term success.
